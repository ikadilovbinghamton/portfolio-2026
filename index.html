The Open Lab and Hands-On Approach

The Open Lab provides a supportive environment where students can work on assignments and labs while asking questions. Immediate access to assistance enhances students' understanding and ability to work through challenges. This hands-on approach (similar to learning a musical instrument) allows students to actively apply what they've learned and practice their skills. While this method is more challenging and requires more preparation time from the instructor, it's ultimately more effective. Practical experience helps students develop their skills, leading to deeper understanding and better knowledge retention.

Project-Based Learning

Project-based learning (PBL) is proven to stimulate student interest and intellectual curiosity by connecting learning to real-world issues, allowing student choice, and integrating multiple subjects. It inspires critical thinking through complex problem-solving, collaborative learning, reflection, feedback, and authentic assessment. By engaging students in meaningful projects, PBL fosters deeper understanding, creativity, and critical analysis, equipping them with essential skills for lifelong learning and real-world problem-solving.

Receiving positive feedback from students about their experiences with project-based learning is incredibly rewarding. Many students express that they feel they have learned the most through these hands-on, practical projects. They appreciate the opportunity to engage with real-world problems, collaborate with peers, and take ownership of their learning.

Students often highlight how these projects make learning more relevant and enjoyable, helping them to understand and retain complex concepts better than traditional methods. The process of researching, problem-solving, and receiving feedback helps them develop critical thinking and creativity, making their educational experience both meaningful and memorable.

Teaching Evidence & Assessment Results

Course Assessment Overview

My course assessment approach measures student learning in a structured, evidence-based way while supporting continuous instructional improvement. Across computer science and information systems courses, I align assessments with course learning outcomes and broader program goals, using multiple measures rather than relying on a single exam or final grade. This approach lets me evaluate both conceptual understanding and applied technical performance across introductory, intermediate, and capstone-level coursework.

Assessment evidence includes quizzes, exams, programming assignments, labs, projects, class activities,and presentations. In introductory courses, assessment focuses on foundational skills such as problem solving, logic, debugging, and readiness for more advanced coursework. In intermediate and advanced courses, assessment emphasizes application, system design, technical integration, and independent problem solving. In capstone courses, assessment extends to project scoping, implementation, testing, documentation, and professional communication.

Formative assessment is an important part of my teaching approach because it allows me to monitor student understanding throughout the learning process rather than waiting until the end of a unit or course. In computer science and information systems courses, I use formative assessments such as low-stakes quizzes, in-class coding exercises, lab checkpoints, draft submissions, discussion prompts, troubleshooting activities, and project progress reviews. These activities help identify misconceptions early, give students timely feedback, and create opportunities to adjust instruction before gaps become larger.

Curriculum Revisions and Assessment

Improving coherence, sequencing, and student readiness

Across multiple annual review cycles, my curriculum work has focused on improving student readiness, strengthening course and program coherence, expanding relevant pathways, and aligning offerings more closely with workforce and transfer needs. This work has included revising the A.A.S. in Computer Programming, redesigning the A.A.S. in Computer Support, contributing to new certificate pathways, and updating courses such as Scripting, HTML, Object-Oriented Programming, Project, Unix/Linux, Database Management, Data Structures, Mobile Application Development, and Introduction to Computer Science.


Throughout these revisions, I simplified student learning outcomes where needed, updated course content, improved sequencing, and developed programs that are both more accessible to students and more sustainable for staff.

Designing flexible structures that support mobility and persistence

A major theme of this work has been using curriculum revision to address observed student challenges. Course assessments in introductory courses repeatedly highlighted low performance, weak preparation for programming coursework, and the need for better alignment across courses within the degree. In response, I helped revise degree structures to reduce unnecessary CPT requirements, improve coherence between courses, and reconsider where foundational courses should appear in the sequence.


In the most recent phase of this work, the revisions became more systems-oriented. The revised A.A.S. Computer Programming and A.A.S. Computer Support programs were intentionally structured so that the first year is shared and roughly half of the coursework overlaps, making it easier for students to switch between programs or complete both. The revisions also considered staffing realities by designing programs that could be delivered more effectively with limited faculty availability.

Moving from reflective practice to structured evidence-based improvement

My assessment work has evolved from reflective teaching practice into a more explicit, outcomes-based model. Early efforts emphasized creating context for learning, using project-based evaluation in skill-based courses, using assignments and tests in knowledge-based courses, and providing formative feedback early and often.

As part of this assessment initiative, I aligned assignments with program goals and course outcomes, used multiple assessment methods such as quizzes, exams, programming assignments, class activities, and projects, and incorporated student feedback to refine instructional strategies. I also analyzed grade distributions and course-level performance patterns to identify gateway-course challenges and support more evidence-based instructional decision-making.


Most recently, I contributed to committee-level assessment leadership by advancing a bottom-up assessment approach that uses student self-evaluation and student learning outcome quizzes mapped to course SLOs and the degree map, with the long-term goal of automating the process through Brightspace.

Temporal and spatial dynamics in higher education


Research Focus

I am pursuing a Ph.D. in Systems Science at Binghamton University, where my doctoral research focuses on a multivariate analysis of temporal and spatial dynamics in higher education. This work examines how temporal change and geographic variation shape institutional performance, student retention, enrollment patterns, and operational efficiency.

Training and Milestones

My doctoral training includes advanced study in systems science, probability and statistics, collective dynamics of complex systems, healthcare systems, healthcare data science and analytics, neural networks, and network science. The learning contract also outlines formal milestones including the comprehensive examination, prospectus colloquium, dissertation preparation, and final oral defense.

Systems Science Framing

A central theme of my dissertation is the application of systems science to higher education as a complex adaptive system. My recent doctoral work includes conceptual and methodological writing on multivariate methods in education, temporal and spatial modeling, and animated geographic visualization of SUNY community colleges from 2015 to 2024.

Publication Pipeline

The dissertation research is organized into a developing publication agenda centered on SUNY community colleges, including work on human capital efficiency and innovation scaling, the influence of economic conditions on retention stability in online versus seated courses, and spatial analysis of enrollment patterns and geographic proximity.
